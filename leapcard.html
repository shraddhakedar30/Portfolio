01 — Research

Understanding how Ireland actually commutes

App Store Rating 3.0/5 Before redesign
Active Users 1M+ Leap Card holders
Play Store Reviews 6.4k User complaints analysed
Key Issues Found 4 Core pain point themes

Research began with 6,400+ Play Store reviews and App Store feedback. Recurring frustrations centred on NFC failures at gates, rigid top-up amounts, no low-balance warnings, and a lack of trust around card security in public. Every design decision in this redesign traces back to a specific complaint observed in this data.

NFC Scan Failures
Users frequently stuck at bus and Luas gates when NFC fails — no fallback option visible in the app.
High Impact
Rigid Top-Up Amounts
Only fixed preset amounts available. Budget users forced to overpay or leave themselves short for their journey.
High Impact
No Low-Balance Alerts
Commuters discover an empty card only at the barrier — too late. No push notifications or proactive reminders exist.
Medium Impact
Security & Trust
Full card numbers visible on screen. No biometric login. Users uncomfortable using the app in crowded public spaces.
Medium Impact

Conclusion
What this project taught me
Redesigning the Leap Card app was an exercise in listening first and designing second. Every decision traces back to something a real user said, felt, or struggled with.
01
Research drives design
Starting with Play Store reviews and user complaints gave the project a factual foundation. The pain points weren't assumed — they were observed. This made every design decision defensible and purposeful
02
Personas ground decisions
Having Jay and Clodagh as reference points prevented feature creep. When a decision felt uncertain, returning to "would this help Jay catch his bus?" instantly clarified the priority.
03
Fallbacks matter more than flow
The most impactful change wasn't the visual redesign — it was the manual entry fallback. Users don't care about aesthetics when NFC fails at a bus gate. Resilient flows beat polished ones.
04
Speed is a feature
For Jay, saving 30 seconds isn't a nice-to-have — it's the difference between catching a bus and missing a lecture. Designing for time pressure changed how I thought about every screen.
Redesigning how ireland commutes
Small friction points at scale become enormous collective frustration. A fallback button, a push notification, a masked card number — the right small changes make a daily essential feel like it actually works for you.
